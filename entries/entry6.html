What Kind of Disabled Are You?

Composed Sunday, February 16, 2025

Preface

"What kind of Disabled are you" is not a question that should be levelled at anyone, especially if you are not Disabled and asking it to someone new who is.

But it is a question that I recently have been asking myself. While I don't believe that self-disclosure of diagnosis should become a widespread practice, I think I owe it to my fellow Disabled practitioners in accessibility, or anyone Disabled who happens upon this blog, to clarify some details about my self-identification and journey. This is especially because there are many places in this blog where I use my Disabled identity as an indicator of some kind of authority.

I am also not opposed to sharing about my journey here. This is not a blog that I anticipate will become my full front-facing professional persona. I retain a degree of anonymity, and I think the details could be helpful to another person in a similar situation.

Because once something's out there, you can never really take it back.

The Disclosure

Enough suspense. I have HMO, or multiple hereditary osteochondromas, or multiple hereditary exostoses, or MHO, or HME, or MHE. It's rare enough that they can't agree on a name.

I like 'HMO' because I am also queer (big surprise).

HMO folks have bumpy bones. A bunch of benign tumours all over the skeleton that restrict movement alongside causing pain and fatigue. My parents tell me I was diagnosed at three years old. My first X-ray revealed bumps on my ribcage "as multitudinous as the stars." Through surgery, I have gotten rid of the tumours that caused movement restrictions. I still get mega fatigue.

It was pretty disruptive throughout my childhood. Things have since changed. You can no longer tell by just looking at me. I used to have a cane on me at all times, either in my hand or folded up in my bag. I have not used such a cane in three years. I could have went cane-less and crutch-less much sooner, except I went through a lot of medical gaslighting through my youth that basically forced me to deal with all of the symptoms on my own with zero intervention.

The medical gaslighting was so bad that I didn't even link my own mobility issues with the HMO diagnosis until I was an adult. I was convinced that I was faking it, or the unfortunate victim of some unsolvable plague, by all the adults around me.

I have also been diagnosed with a few mental illnesses (I don't think I am obliged to say which) that still interfere with my everyday, but they used to be worse in childhood. While I don't have an official diagnosis for any cognitive conditions, the mechanism underlying HMO (heparan sulfate deficiency) is speculated to cause "autism-like" behaviour, and I have been peer-diagnosed several times, including by my own therapist. The waitlist to get an actual diagnosis through the public medical system is over two years long.

Everything Was Worse in Childhood

This is the reason why I felt the need to make this post and this disclosure. As an adult, I have not really had to struggle for access in the same way I did as a child.

But as a child, I had no political awareness or identity as a Disabled person. My political awareness and Disabled identity were formed retrospectively after the storm had subsided.

This has caused a fundamental contradiction at the heart of all the activism I do. I used to struggle massively if a door couldn't open automatically. I can now open it with ease, but I still sometimes talk like I'm my past self.

The past is incredibly sticky. And like some horrific goo, it is surprisingly malleable. The authority I derive from my traumatic memories carries more reliability than a person who just dreams up whatever scenario, or participates in a "disability simulator" (don't even get me started), but it is not on the same level as a person giving live testimony from what it is like to be physically Disabled person today experiencing perpetual access barriers.

What Kind of Disabled Am I?

I've had a very unique journey. The kind of Disabled person I am has changed throughout my life, just as the kinds of disabilities I am impacted by day-to-day have also changed.

I am still coming to terms with my own neurodivergencies, as well as fully recognizing the extent to which my mental illnesses impact me.
